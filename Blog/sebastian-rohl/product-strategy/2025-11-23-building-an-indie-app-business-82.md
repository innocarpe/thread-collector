Hello again and welcome to my weekly indie log! Right now, almost all my focus is on building my new Pomodoro timer app, because I’m hoping to finally release version 1 already next week. In this issue, I want to share what happened in these last intense days before the launch, what last things I worked on, what went well, and where I had a few struggles. Let me give you a look into my week and all the steps I took to get closer to the finish line.

This week I started beta testing the app. I invited about 30 people, including family, friends, and some people I know from X and LinkedIn, who I knew would be interested and willing to test it out. When I started getting the feedback, I felt a bit overwhelmed by the amount and the different opinions, but after a while I sat down and made a big list, organized all the points, and tried to decide which comments are important for version 1. That helped a lot to see more clearly what needs to be fixed or improved before launch.

The great thing was that nobody found a really bad bug, like one that crashes the app or loses your data. Most feedback was about the design or how to use the app, and honestly, this is the best kind of feedback because it helps you think about how people actually use it in real life. These are the moments where you can make the app much smoother and nicer, and I really enjoyed going through these ideas to make the user experience better.

One thing that almost all my beta testers mentioned was that adjusting the default timer was too hidden. Before, you could only change it in the settings, which many people didn’t find very practical. Another comment I got a lot was the missing option to add a couple of minutes to a session, even after it finished. I realized these are things that real users expect from a good Pomodoro app, and when I thought about it, it made a lot of sense to improve this before the big launch.

Actually, I originally planned to add this for version 1.1 later, but after all the feedback, I took a day and quickly implemented it for the first version. I’m glad I changed my plans, because now the app feels much better and more “complete”. It’s cool to see that listening to feedback and reacting fast can make such a difference. I hope the testers will also be happy with the improvements!

Something that really gives me hope for this app is that my post looking for beta testers on X got a lot of attention. It almost reached 30,000 views and had more than 250 likes, which is much more than I usually get. For me, this feels like quite a big deal, and it makes me think that there might really be some interest in my app out there. It was a great feeling to see so many people reacting and even sending me messages to try out the beta. That’s by the way exactly how the success story of HabitKit started!

All of this gives me more motivation to finally release version 1. I am honestly very excited to get it out there, start with some marketing, and see what people think about it. I am already thinking about what new features for future updates I should focus on. For me, getting something to version 1 is always the most difficult step. Once there is a first release out, everything feels a lot easier because you can just keep improving and building on what is already there.

There was one pretty big problem I ran into this week. When I started working on this app about three months ago, I already picked a name that I really liked and felt would fit nicely in my app portfolio. I thought everything was fine and got used to it, but now, just before I want to launch, I found out that there is another new app with the same name in the app stores. This really surprised me, and now I’m quite unsure what to do. I don’t want users to be confused by two apps with the same name, and of course I also don’t want to “take over” someone else’s project name by accident. At the same time, I feel a bit attached to the name, because it fits so well with my idea and the rest of my apps.

Now, it is only a few days before my planned release, and this makes the whole situation a bit stressful. I even tried to find and contact the developer of the other app, because I thought maybe we can somehow agree or maybe I can buy the name, but I couldn’t find any way to reach out to him online. So, if I can’t find a solution very soon, I think I will be forced to rename my app, even if I don’t really want to. This is a bit annoying, but I guess it’s better in the long run to have an original name and no conflicts with other apps.

One of the biggest things that could make me miss my goal of releasing next week is being stuck in the Apple review process. I already submitted a first version for review on Friday, just to see how it goes and whether everything is fine. But right now, it’s still waiting in review and honestly, I don’t think it will move forward today anymore. Maybe Apple will look at it on Monday, but you never really know with them. I just hope they don’t find anything that makes them reject it or send it back to me.

Before sending it in, I made sure to double-check my paywall and change it so that it follows their guidelines. Someone on X actually messaged me and told me there was a problem with how the prices for the yearly subscription were shown. I hope I fixed it well, because you never know exactly what Apple wants or what details they care about. So now I’m just waiting and hoping that everything is okay and I don’t run into new problems with the review. 

I also created the App Store screenshots for the app. Instead of using Figma, I used Paper this time and I really like it as an alternative for creating designs. I tried to keep the look of the screenshots in sync with the design of the app and I am pretty happy with the results for now.
