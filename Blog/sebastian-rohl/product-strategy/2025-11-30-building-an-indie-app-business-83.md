I did it! I managed to stick to my own deadline and released my new app, FocusKit, on the iOS App Store. It feels amazing to finally have it out there after all the hard work of the last weeks. If you want to check it out, you can [download FocusKit now](https://apps.apple.com/us/app/pomodoro-timer-focuskit/id6752217216), and if you like it, I would really appreciate a review. Good reviews help a lot with getting the app in front of more people and make a big difference for a small indie developer like me.

Thanks to everyone who supported the launch, I really appreciate it!

For everyone interested in the numbers, here is a quick summary: I launched FocusKit yesterday, and on the first day it already made $111 in revenue and brought in $6 in monthly recurring revenue (MRR). Honestly, I think this is a good start for a brand new app. I feel quite happy seeing these first results and it gives me hope that maybe I can turn FocusKit into a real business over time. My goal is to slowly grow these numbers and see where I can take it.

Now, Here is everything about the final stretches of development and the launch: 

I started the Apple review process even before last weekend. My plan was to hand in the app as early as possible, so I would have enough time in case anything went wrong. I think this was a smart move, because the first two submissions actually got rejected by Apple for reasons that were not easy to understand. They told me it was because of “Guideline 4.3(a) - Design - Spam”, which made me really confused because I tried to make FocusKit unique and useful. 
[이미지: Image]

After that, there were a few days with lots of back and forth with Apple. I had to explain in detail what makes my app different from other apps and show them that FocusKit is not spam. It took some effort, but finally they approved my app and I could continue to prepare the launch. In the end, having only two rejections for a new app is not too bad, so I am quite happy how it worked out in the end.

With all these steps finished, I was finally ready for a weekend launch without any big problems getting in the way. I decided to launch on Saturday the 29th instead of Sunday, because I actually had some private appointments on Sunday that I had forgotten about before. Maybe this was even a better choice, because I started to think that more people might try a focus timer app on a Saturday compared to a Sunday. On Saturdays, some people might use their free time to get things done, so it could even help the launch a bit. In the end, it felt like everything worked out for a good and timely release.

On Friday morning, I already pressed the Release button in App Store Connect. My idea was to do a soft launch first, mostly to test if everything works as expected and see if there are any problems, especially with the in-app purchases. I spent some time going through the app again and checked all the features and payments to make sure nothing important was broken. Luckily, I didn’t find any errors or issues that would stop the launch, so I felt ready and confident for the real launch on Saturday.

Here are some things I always do on a launch day:

One of the first things I always do is posting about the launch on social media. I have active profiles on X, LinkedIn, Bluesky, and Threads, so I cross-post my updates to all these platforms. Even though my followers are probably not all Pomodoro timer fans, it’s still the best way to give the app an initial push, get some first downloads, and hopefully collect some useful feedback. I don’t expect to reach huge numbers there, but usually a few people get curious, try out the app, and maybe even share their thoughts or give a review, which really helps me to improve and get started.

Every time I launch a new app, I take some time to message my friends who have supported me during these last three years of building in public. I found out that it really helps to have a small group of people who you can count on. Usually, a few of them are happy to download the app right away and leave a nice review, which can make a big difference in the early days. Good reviews are super important because they help the app get noticed and move up in the App Store rankings. I think having this kind of support is something every indie developer needs, especially when starting from zero.

One of the first things I did after launching was to set up an Apple Search Ads campaign. As soon as FocusKit was available in the App Store, I created some ads for the most relevant keywords that people might use to find a focus timer app. My budget was just $200, which isn’t much, but from what I’ve seen and heard, it can be a good way to help with App Store Optimization and to kickstart your first downloads. Even if it doesn’t bring a huge amount of users, it’s still a nice way to get the app showing up more and maybe get some new people to try it out. In the worst case, I’ll spend the money and only get a few downloads, but at least it’s some extra attention for the launch.

One of my most important tasks after launch is to watch out for feedback from users. Honestly, I always hope and cross my fingers that nobody finds a big bug that could crash the app or even make people lose their data. Luckily, that didn’t happen this time. Of course, if something serious like this comes up, I fix it as fast as possible. When I get feedback with new ideas or things to improve, I make sure to write everything down in my backlog. Right now, my list is already full of great suggestions and new features I could add to FocusKit. I want to keep working on the app to make it better and better with every update. I have a really good feeling about FocusKit and hope that it will find its own place in the world of Pomodoro timer apps.

After all the hard work, I also like to celebrate a little bit when I launch a new app. My personal tradition is to order a big pizza and enjoy it while I read the first feedback and reviews from users. It feels really good to finally relax for a moment, knowing that I managed to reach my goal and bring the app live. For me, this small celebration is a nice way to remind myself to be proud of what I achieved, even if there are still many things to do next.

Even though the app is now live, there is still a lot of work ahead of me. I want to reach out to some press websites and maybe get someone to write about FocusKit, and I also want to try running some ads on Reddit to see if that brings in new users. At the same time, I have many ideas for new features and ways to improve the user experience, so I’m already planning the next updates. I also want to write some blog posts where I share more about the development and what I learned. Doing all of this by myself can feel a bit much and sometimes it gets pretty hectic, but I try my best to keep up. 

Besides FocusKit, I also have to work on some important updates for HabitKit, so I have to split my time wisely. Even if it’s stressful, I still enjoy working on my own apps and trying to make them better every week.

Come back next week if you want to see how FocusKit is going and what steps I am taking to help it grow. I’ll share all the updates and let you know what’s working and what I am learning along the way.
