Let’s be honest: most of us devs love to jump straight into building. We get a spark of an idea, feel that itch in our fingers, and suddenly… we’re three days deep into coding something that might not even be needed.

Been there. Way too often.

In this post, I want to walk you through how I validate my app ideas **before** I start implementing them. I’ll share where I get my ideas from, what I did wrong in the past, how I approach validation today especially using **ASO tools **and the impact it had on my recent projects. You’ll even see a real example of my process.

### 🔍 Where My App Ideas Come From

Honestly, there’s no secret sauce. I get my ideas from **talking to people**. Friends, meetups, casual chats. Sometimes someone mentions a problem and my brain instantly starts working on “how could I build something for that?”

I also get a ton of inspiration from **Twitter**. Following indie hackers and product builders often sparks ideas I wouldn’t have thought of myself. Same goes for certain **YouTube channels** where creators discuss startup concepts sometimes even failed ones, which are often more valuable.

Occasionally, friends call me and pitch me their “million dollar app idea.” It happens, but it’s more the exception than the rule. Most ideas actually come from **observing pain points** or seeing apps that exist and thinking:

> _“I could do this better.”_

And that’s totally fine. You don’t have to reinvent the wheel. Improving an existing idea is often the more realistic and profitable path.

### ❌ What I Did Wrong in the Past

In my earlier projects, I’d get an idea and just go for it. No validation. Just vibes.

I got **too emotionally attached**, spent days, weeks, even months building things I _thought_ people needed. I even showed it to friends and heard things like:

> _“Yeah! This is definitely a good idea!” …without them ever needing it themselves or proving it._

**The result?** Some of those apps ended up with **zero downloads**. No traction, no users, no feedback, nothing. And that’s a hard pill to swallow when you’ve put so much energy into something.

### ✅ How I Validate Ideas Today

These days, I take a much more practical approach especially since I mostly build **mobile apps**.

The main tool in my validation toolbox is **ASO (App Store Optimization)**. Here’s what I do:

I use ASO tools to research **keywords** related to the idea.

I check:

- Are people searching for this?
- Is there demand?
- How popular is the keyword?
- What’s the competition level?

If a keyword has **high popularity** and **low competition**, that’s a strong signal the idea might work. But I don’t stop there.

I look at **existing apps** that use those keywords. If their apps have been around for a while and still only have **1,000–3,000 reviews**, even with high keyword popularity… something’s off. That usually tells me there’s a gap — but not necessarily one worth building into.

When it comes to SaaS products, I take a different route. I create a **question list** based on the pain point, and then I post it on **Twitter** to see how people respond.

Why not ask friends? Because friends mean well — but that’s the problem. They’ll try to **support you**, not necessarily **tell you the truth**. Random people on Twitter? Much more honest. They don’t know you, they have no reason to sugarcoat things. That’s the feedback you want.

### 🔢 Real Example: Using ASO to Validate

Let’s say I want to build a **smoking reduction app** (which I actually did).

I go into an ASO tool and start checking keywords like:

- “quit smoking”
- “stop smoking app”
- “smoking tracker”
- “how to quit smoking”

I check the **popularity score** — let’s say “quit smoking” is at 45. That’s solid. Then I look at the **difficulty score** — if it’s over 50, I think twice. Over 70? Probably not worth it unless you’re going big with ads.
Keywords for my Smoke Free App

Then I check what apps rank under that keyword. If the top apps only have a few thousand reviews after years on the store, that’s a red flag. But if they have 20K+ reviews, and I can offer a **different angle**, I might have something.

That’s exactly what I did for my **Smoke Free App** — and the result? I saw way more **impressions**, **downloads**, and even **first paid users** after launch. That was the moment I knew this new validation approach works.

### ⛔ Some Bad Examples to Watch Out For

Not every keyword that looks good at first glance is worth pursuing.

Take a keyword with **high popularity** but **also very high competition**. Even if people are searching for it, you’re up against top apps with massive ad budgets. Without serious marketing spend, it’s going to be very difficult to get any visibility.

Another example: I checked the keyword **“bike repair”**. The keyword itself had decent popularity, but when I looked at the top 10 apps ranking for it, most had fewer than **400 reviews**. That’s a big red flag. It tells me there aren’t many people actively looking for a solution in this space and even existing apps aren’t gaining much traction. That’s probably not a market worth entering.

These kinds of checks help avoid wasting weeks on something that has little or no demand.

### 🚀 Conclusion

Since I started validating my ideas this way, my projects are actually getting traction. I get more impressions, more downloads, and most importantly **real users**.

Of course, I’m still human. I still have apps I’m emotionally attached to. I still update a few projects even though I _know_they’re not going anywhere. But I’m getting better at spotting those red flags early.

So if you’re like me and love building things:

> _Do yourself a favor. Validate it first. Because building the wrong thing perfectly… is still the wrong thing._
